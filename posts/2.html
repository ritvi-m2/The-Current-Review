How Neuromorphic AI is Redefining Computing

December 19, 2025

The human brain is the most efficient in the universe. Our minds are able to recognise complex patterns, subconsciously process language, and coordinate our muscles and nerves to move, and yet, the brain only consumes about 20 watts of power (about the same as your average lightbulb!). On the opposite end of the power spectrum, the world’s recent advancements in AI require GPU clusters that consume over 100,000 times the amount of power our brain uses. This has led engineers to the conclusion that AI needs to be built less like machinery and more like brains; this is the foundation of Neuromorphic Engineering.

Breaking the von Neumann Bottleneck

Almost every computer since the 1940s utilises the von Neumann architecture; it separates the thinking (processor) from the data (memory). Whenever a calculation needs to be done, data must be transmitted back and forth between the processor and memory via buses. These transmissions consume the majority of a chip’s energy.

Diagram detailing how data moves in the Von Neumann architecture

In neuromorphic chips (such as Intel’s Loihi or IBM’s TrueNorth), the thinking and data are collocated in the form of digital “neurons” and “synapses.” This allows the complete negation of any form of data transmission, eliminating the majority of unnecessary energy consumption and allowing for a system that is up to 10,000 times more efficient than the von Neumann architecture.

Spiking Neural Networks: The Logic of Life

While traditional AI functions on continuous mathematical values, neuromorphic systems operate on Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs). Similar to the human nervous system, SNNs transmit information via discrete “spikes” of electricity. In a traditional processor, every part of the chip is active at all times and consumes power after every clock cycle. In an SNN, a “neuron” only fires a spike when it receives sufficient input; otherwise, it is essentially dormant and consumes no power. For situations such as autonomous vehicles and prosthetic limbs where power conservation is critical, this approach is a perfect fit.

Diagram displaying a model of Spiking Neural Networks

Real-World Applications

Currently, most devices like Alexa and Siri are just interfaces; the actual processing happens elsewhere in a huge data centre. However, neuromorphic chips would allow all of the processing to occur locally on the device itself. Imagine a medical chip that could detect early signs of a seizure by analysing brain waves on the spot, or a drone that can scan a dense forest using just the power from a small battery. Neuromorphic chips would not only allow for instant calculations with no need to store data in a cloud, but they are also, unlike standard AI models, dynamic, and can “learn” and adapt to new environments on the spot.

A New Era of Engineering

The goal of engineering is shifting from “how powerful can we make this?” to “how efficient can we make this?” Engineering chips isn’t about forcing a machine to process numbers swiftly; it’s about imitating biology.
