Graphene Chips: Why They Can Outperform Silicon

December 5, 2025

For 50 years, the history of computing has traditionally been written in silicon. From the first circuits to the multi-billion-transistor processors powering today's computers, silicon has been the standard for chips. However, we have now reached the ‘post-silicon’ era due to physical necessities. For decades, the semiconductor industry has relied on the scalability of silicon; however, at the 2nm scale, physics begins to limit its capabilities. We now face issues of quantum tunnelling and thermal runaway, which are problems that more cooling cannot solve.

To move past this ceiling, the industry is pivoting toward graphene: a single-atom-thick lattice of carbon. This isn't just a marginal upgrade, but a shift in how we deal with electron transport.

The Architecture of Speed

Graphene is a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice, appearing as a simple honeycomb structure. However, its electrical properties leave silicon in the dust due to its carrier mobility.

The structure of graphene

In traditional silicon transistors, electrons constantly collide with silicon atoms. This loses energy as the heat band slows down the electronics. In graphene, however, electrons behave as massless ‘Dirac fermions’. This means they move through the lattice with essentially no resistance and can even reach speeds nearly 100 times faster than in silicon. While current high-end consumer CPUs struggle to maintain clock speeds above 6GHz without super-cooling, a graphene-based processor could theoretically operate in the Terahertz range.

Thermal Bottleneck

As you may have experienced while gaming, programming or other CPU-intensive tasks, heat is a major issue in terms of performance. In modern chip design, we are not limited by the number of transistors we can fit, but by how much heat we can remove before the chip melts; a concept known as ‘Thermal Design Power’ (TDP).

Graphene is one of the most thermally conductive materials known to researchers and even significantly outperforms copper and diamond. By utilising graphene in chip architecture, we not only get a faster processor, but we also get one that dissipates heat efficiently. This would allow for the stacking of chips, placing layers of processors on top of each other, without the bottom layers overheating. As a result, this means thinner designs, longer battery lives and no noisy cooling fans in mobile devices and laptops.

The Bandgap

The reason graphene isn't already in our devices is the result of an integral property of semiconductors: the bandgap. To function as a switch, a material must be able to turn “off”. Silicon has a natural energy gap that allows it to stop the flow of electrons. On the other hand, graphene is a zero-overlap semimetal, hence it is always “on”. To researchers, this was known as the “Graphene Wall”. Scientists have tried doping the material with chemicals or cutting it into incredibly narrow nanoribbons to force a bandgap, but these methods often ruined the mobility that made graphene special.

However, at the Georgia Institute of Technology, a massive breakthrough recently occurred. Researchers successfully created a functional “epigraphene” semiconductor by growing graphene on silicon carbide wafers. This created a stable bandgap while maintaining the material’s high mobility. This breakthrough may be the link that moves graphene from laboratories to a viable silicon successor.

Georgia Tech's physics professor holds a graphene device grown on a silicon carbide substrate chip.

The Path Forward

Transitioning the global semiconductor industry from silicon to graphene will not occur overnight. However, the shift is becoming an economic necessity. As we look to the future, graphene is more than a theoretical alternative and is the most promising candidate for the next generation of high-performance computing.
