How Do Photonic Chips Work?

January 2, 2026

The world of engineering is approaching the limit of silicon chips; electrons are too slow and generate too much heat to suffice for the next generation of data-heavy applications. As an electron moves through copper wire, it generates too much thermal energy and encounters too much resistance, bottlenecking new, advanced processors. Instead of moving electrons faster, engineers intend to replace them with photons, birthing Photonic Integrated Circuits (PICs).

From Electrons to Photons: The Physics of Bandwidth

Photonic chips are designed to complete the same tasks as electron chips; they just use light instead of electrons. Photons, unlike electrons, have no mass and no charge, meaning they can travel at the speed of light without causing any resistance or heating issues that occur in traditional semiconductors. In a standard CPU, increasing clock speed requires an exponential increase in power consumption; the energy required to transmit a signal in a photonic system has almost no correlation to the distance it needs to cover or how much speed it needs to have.

However, there is a problem. Engineers have already managed to manipulate light in fibre-optic cables, but those are over kilometres of distance. Doing the same on a tiny chip the size of a coin is a different story.

The Building Blocks: Waveguides and Modulators

To build a circuit out of light instead of electricity, we need the optical equivalents of wires and transistors.

Waveguides: The optical replacement of wires. They use the concept of Total Internal Reflection to entrap light in a microscopic channel. Engineers precisely measure and control the refractive indexes of the materials to guide light around corners with extreme accuracy and precision. Waveguides are normally made of silicon or silicon nitrate.

Optical Modulators: To represent 0s and 1s, the properties of the light must be altered to signal the difference. Optical modulators modify the intensity, phase, or polarization of the beam. Modern Mach-Zehnder Interferometers (MZIs) work by splitting the light beam in half, and shifting the phase of one of the halves to produce either constructive or destructive interference.

Photodetectors: After all the processing and computation, at the end of the circuit, the light will need to be reconstructed into an electrical signal that a computer can process. Photodetectors can convert billions of optical pulses back into electrical signals per second.

The 2026 Breakthrough: Co-Packaged Optics (CPO)

A conceptual model of Co-Packaged Optics (CPO), showing optical engines integrated directly onto the processor substrate to reduce power consumption and increase bandwidth

In the new year, the industry has moved beyond simply “plugging in” optical cables into servers. By placing the photonic “optical engine” directly onto the same substrate as the CPU or GPU, we eliminate a lot of energy consumption that is usually required to connect them. This is what allows the latest data centres for AI to handle the models with trillions of parameters that require immense amounts of power.
