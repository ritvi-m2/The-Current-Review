Beyond Lithium Batteries: Why Sodium is the Future

January 30, 2026

Lithium ion batteries have been the standard for portable electronics and electric vehicles, so much so that the industry is said to be hitting a “lithium ceiling”. Between exploding demand, volatile pricing and ethical concerns regarding mining, our search for a sustainable alternative has led us to sodium.

The Problem with Lithium

Lithium is rare, making up only 0.002% of the Earth’s crust. Extraction is often concentrated in geopolitically sensitive areas (the “Lithium Triangle”) and requires large quantities of water. As we transition to a fully green grid, we cannot mine lithium efficiently or quickly enough to meet global demand.

Advantages of the Sodium-Ion

By contrast, sodium is the sixth most abundant element on Earth and is found in vast quantities in seawater and ash deposits around the world. This geographic ubiquity mitigates the risks of resource nationalism and significantly reduces the carbon footprint of transporting raw materials across the world.

Similarities and Divergence

Na-ion batteries operate on the same rocking chair principle as Li-ion batteries at a fundamental level. Ions move from the cathode to the anode during charging and back during discharging. However, the physical properties of the sodium ion create distinct challenges and advantages.

The sodium ion is larger and heavier than lithium and has historically struggled with intercalation, fitting into the structure of the electrode. The first sodium batteries suffered from rapid degradation. But the development of hard carbon anodes has revolutionised the field. Hard carbon has a disordered structure with wider layers, which can comfortably accommodate the larger sodium ions and allow for a longer life.

Relationship with Aluminium

One of the most significant advantages of sodium is its relationship with aluminium. In Li-ion batteries, the anode has to use copper as a current collector due to the fact that lithium alloys with aluminium at low voltages, destroying the battery. Copper is more expensive and heavier than aluminium.

On the other hand, sodium does not alloy with aluminium, which allows manufacturers to replace heavy, expensive copper foil with cheap, lightweight aluminium foil for both the cathode and anode. This switch may produce a significant reduction in the bill of materials and has been cited as a 20% to 30% total cost saving over lithium-ion.

Safety, Logistics, and Temperature

Sodium batteries offer safety that lithium cannot replicate. One major hazard in shipping lithium batteries is their thermal runaway risk; they must be shipped at 30% charge. Sodium batteries can be fully discharged to zero volts for transport, which makes them chemically inert during shipping. This eliminates the fire hazard as well as simplifies international transport logistics.

Furthermore, sodium batteries outperform in extreme climates. While lithium batteries lose capacity significantly and struggle to charge in minus temperatures, Na-ion cells maintain over 90% of their capacity at extreme minus temperatures. This makes them ideal for grid storage in cold regions.
