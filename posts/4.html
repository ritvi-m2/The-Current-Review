Quantum Computing: A Step Closer

January 16, 2026

For decades, quantum computing has been a huge grey area between physics and technology. It remained a theoretical playground for physicists and an impossibility for engineers. However, in 2026, this has shifted from abstract principles to concrete challenges of error correction and systems engineering. Scientists no longer wonder if quantum computers may work, but how we can scale them.

The Qubit Modality War

As of now, there isn't yet a standard for quantum hardware. Instead, we see fierce competition between different physical modalities, each with its own trade-offs:

IBM and Google rely on LC circuits, which are cooled to temperatures colder than outer space. The issue here is the dilution refrigerator and the complicated wiring required to control qubits without heating them up.

Companies like IonQ utilise individual atoms suspended in vacuums by electromagnetic fields. These have a much higher fidelity (accuracy) than superconducting chips, but are slower and harder to integrate into a compact form or device.

The Era of Logical Qubits

This year, researchers have begun the transition from physical qubits to logical qubits. In the NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) era, a single vibration could cause a qubit to decohere and lose its data. Engineers now use error correction codes where hundreds of fragile physical qubits are bundled together to form a single, stable logical qubit. This solves the issue of qubit decohesion.

Diagram of physical qubits vs logical qubits

In early 2026, researchers successfully demonstrated surface codes that can detect and fix errors in real time. Reliable logical qubits allow us to finally run algorithms like Shor’s or Grover’s that will redefine cryptography and materials science.

Hybrid Workflows: The Quantum Accelerator

There is a common misconception that quantum computers will replace everyday devices such as laptops. In reality, the future is likely to be hybrid. At institutions such as Purdue and UMD, researchers are developing Quantum Accelerators, which are highly specialised modules that work alongside classical supercomputers. The classical CPU handles the logic and user interface, while the quantum chip is called to solve complex problems such as simulating structures of battery cathode or optimising global shipping routes.

Quantum computing is moving out of the physics lab and onto the engineering floor. The challenge for the next decade isn't just discovering new physics, but building the robust, scalable infrastructure that can house the most delicate machines ever created.
